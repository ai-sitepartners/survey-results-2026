Key Insights

Industry is in Early-to-Mid Adoption Phase

The average AI maturity score of 4.3/10 indicates most organizations are still exploring and experimenting with AI. The majority (53%) rate their maturity between 3-6, suggesting widespread awareness but limited systematic implementation.

  • Only 8% have reached "AI-Embedded" status (maturity 9-10)
  • 22% are still in early exploration (maturity 0-2)
  • Peak distribution at level 3 (19% of responses) indicates active experimentation
  • Most organizations describe themselves as using AI for "individual tasks" rather than "organizational workflows"

Strong Investment Appetite Despite Barriers

With an average investment likelihood of 5.7/10, organizations show clear intent to increase AI adoption. 48% rated their likelihood at 7 or higher, signaling significant growth potential in the coming year.

  • 11% rate investment likelihood at perfect 10/10 - these are the committed innovators
  • Only 16% show low interest (ratings 0-2), suggesting AI skepticism is minimal
  • 18% sit at neutral (rating 5), likely waiting for clearer ROI or peer adoption
  • Positive correlation between current maturity and investment intent - success breeds more investment

Tool Fragmentation and Strategy Gaps

While ChatGPT dominates (52% primary usage), followed by Microsoft Copilot (32%), adoption is largely individual-driven rather than organization-wide. The most cited barrier is "Lack of internal strategy" (mentioned in 45% of responses).

  • Organizations lack unified AI tools - employees choose their own solutions
  • Paid ChatGPT users outnumber free users, showing willingness to invest personally
  • Microsoft Copilot benefits from existing M365 deployments but trails in engagement
  • Only 3 respondents primarily use Claude, despite its strong capabilities
  • "None" as primary tool indicates 6% haven't started AI adoption at all
The construction industry is at a critical inflection point: awareness is high, tools are available, but systematic implementation and strategic guidance remain the primary gaps holding back transformation.

Demographic Insights

Contractor-Heavy Sample

General and Specialty Contractors make up 51% of responses, providing strong representation from the construction execution side. Engineering firms (11%) and Architecture firms (4%) are also well represented.

  • General Contractors (33%): Large and mid-size firms handling full project delivery
  • Specialty Contractors (20%): Trade-specific expertise (electrical, steel, mechanical, etc.)
  • Engineering Firms (11%): Design and technical consulting perspectives
  • Developer/Owners (10%): Client-side view of AI value proposition
  • Consultants (5%): Advisory and specialized services
  • Representation from ancillary sectors: insurance, technology, manufacturing, distribution

Decision-Maker Perspective

43% of respondents are in senior leadership (C-Suite/Partners and VPs/Directors), offering strategic insights into organizational AI priorities and investment decisions.

  • Managers (40%): Operational leaders making daily technology decisions
  • VP/Directors (26%): Department heads with budget authority
  • Individual Contributors (18%): Hands-on users providing ground truth
  • C-Suite/Partners (17%): Strategic decision-makers setting vision
  • Balance between strategic (43% senior) and tactical (58% manager + IC) perspectives
  • Senior skew suggests AI is Board-level discussion topic, not just IT department concern

Small to Mid-Size Focus

63% of organizations have fewer than 200 employees, suggesting the findings are particularly relevant for SMBs navigating AI adoption without enterprise resources.

  • 1-50 employees (42%): Small firms seeking competitive advantages
  • 51-200 (21%): Growing companies building systematic processes
  • 201-1,000 (19%): Mid-market firms with dedicated tech resources
  • 1,001-10,000 (13%): Large enterprises with complex stakeholder needs
  • 10,000+ (5%): Industry giants setting trends and standards
  • SMB majority faces resource constraints but also gains agility advantages
  • Larger firms represented enough to show enterprise AI adoption patterns

AI Maturity Distribution (0-10)

Maturity Segments

Maturity Analysis

Bell Curve Centered on Exploration

The distribution peaks at maturity level 3 (19% of responses), with 69% of organizations between levels 2-6. This suggests most are past awareness and into active experimentation, but haven't achieved systematic implementation.

  • Classic adoption curve: early adopters (8%), early majority (31%), late majority (37%), laggards (24%)
  • Maturity 3 peak indicates "testing phase" - using AI but not systematically
  • Sharp drop-off after level 6 suggests difficulty scaling from pilots to production
  • Only 3% rated themselves at 0 (complete non-adoption)
  • Distribution skews right - more organizations moving forward than stalling

Why Organizations Rate Themselves Low

Common themes from open responses:

  • "Individual use, not organizational adoption" - Most frequent explanation. Employees experiment personally but company hasn't standardized
  • "No clear strategy or implementation plan" - Using AI reactively, not strategically
  • "Limited to basic tasks, no automation" - Stuck in "ChatGPT for emails" stage
  • "Still piloting and testing, unclear on value" - Exploration without commitment
  • "Privacy and security concerns limiting use" - Want to do more but blocked by risk fears
  • "Not integrated with our core tools" - AI sits outside workflow, reducing impact
  • "Lack of training and understanding" - Team doesn't know how to use AI effectively
  • "Minimal budget allocated" - Interested but not investing meaningfully

The AI-Embedded Leaders (Maturity 9-10)

Only 8% reach maturity levels of 9-10. These organizations typically have dedicated AI strategies, provide organization-wide tool access, have integrated AI into core workflows, and view AI as foundational to operations.

  • Dedicated AI teams or champions driving adoption across departments
  • Organization-wide tool licenses (not just for requesters)
  • AI integrated into core systems (estimating, BIM, project management)
  • Investment likelihood 8-10 - they're doubling down on what works
  • Custom AI solutions beyond consumer tools like ChatGPT
  • Clear ROI measurement and success metrics
  • One respondent noted: "We are AI native! Developing AI-enabled SaaS"
  • These leaders set benchmarks others aim to reach

The Gap from 4 to 7: Crossing the Chasm

The biggest challenge facing the industry is moving from experimentation (levels 3-4) to systematic implementation (levels 7-8). This requires strategy, training, and integration work that most organizations haven't tackled.

  • Drop from 37 organizations at levels 3-4 to 22 organizations at levels 7-8
  • Represents shift from "individuals using tools" to "processes embedding AI"
  • Requires: budget commitment, change management, technical integration, governance
  • Organizations in 5-6 range (31 responses) are "stuck in the middle"
  • Those who cross this chasm become competitive differentiators in their market

Tool Adoption Insights

ChatGPT Dominates Individual Usage

52% of respondents primarily use ChatGPT, with a strong preference for paid versions (51% paid vs 34% free among ChatGPT users). However, organizational provision is fragmented: 35% say their org offers ChatGPT, but often only to those who request it or senior leadership.

  • Personal investment high: Users paying $20/month themselves shows strong perceived value
  • Organizational deployment inconsistent: Only 23% provide licenses to all employees
  • "Available for those who need it" model: Most common approach (35%)
  • Free tier limitations drive upgrades: Users hit rate limits and pay for Plus
  • Top use: proposal writing (58%) - high-value, time-consuming task
  • Meeting minutes generation (42%) - administrative efficiency gain
  • Presentation creation (39%) - content structuring and design support

Microsoft Copilot Gains Through Enterprise Deployment

32% primarily use Copilot, benefiting from existing Microsoft 365 relationships. Organizations with Copilot are more likely to provide it broadly (45% offer licenses to all employees vs 23% for ChatGPT), but paid adoption is lower than ChatGPT.

  • Enterprise M365 integration makes deployment easier for IT departments
  • Broader organizational rollout: 45% provide to all employees (vs 23% for ChatGPT)
  • Lower individual paid adoption: Users rely on company-provided access
  • Security/compliance advantage: Stays within M365 tenant boundaries
  • Integration with Office apps: Works within Word, Excel, Teams, Outlook
  • Free tier competition: Many organizations start with free Copilot in Edge/Bing
  • Learning curve criticism: Users report Copilot "lags behind ChatGPT in accuracy"
  • One user noted: "It can get things wrong but then continue making the exact same mistake"

Top Use Cases: Communication Over Automation

Most common functions across all tools:

  • Drafting proposals and project documentation (58%): RFPs, SOWs, contracts - high-stakes writing
  • Generating meeting minutes and summaries (42%): Converting conversations to action items
  • Preparing presentations and reports (39%): Slide content, executive summaries
  • Spreadsheet analysis and estimating support (25%): Formula help, data interpretation
  • Writing and editing correspondence (22%): Emails, RFIs, responses
  • Safety briefings and toolbox talks (18%): Compliance documentation
  • Training and onboarding materials (15%): New hire documentation
  • Marketing and business development content (14%): Website copy, case studies
  • Creating technical reports or specifications (12%): Engineering documentation
  • Research into codes and standards (10%): Regulatory compliance queries

Notable absence: Very few mention core technical work like BIM integration, design automation, workflow automation, or predictive analytics. These require specialized tools and deeper implementation.

What's Annoying About AI Tools?

Top frustrations cited by users:

  • "Hallucinations and inaccurate information" (Most common complaint) - AI makes up facts, requires verification
  • "Can't use company data due to privacy concerns" - Biggest value locked away by security fears
  • "Inconsistent outputs, needs refinement" - Quality varies, requires multiple iterations
  • "Forced integration and learning curve" - Copilot users frustrated by mandatory adoption
  • "Limited capacity in enterprise/secure versions" - Internal ChatGPT deployments underpowered
  • "Difficulty with prompting and describing needs" - Users lack prompt engineering skills
  • "Doesn't integrate with our tools" - Procore, Revit, BIM 360 not AI-accessible
  • "Still very manual from input perspective" - Copy-paste workflows, not automation
  • "Nothing integrates directly into all programs" - Tool fragmentation problem
  • One user: "Just remembering to use it as a primary resource" - adoption habit challenge

Biggest Barriers to AI Adoption

Barrier Analysis

#1 Barrier: Strategy Gap (45%)

"Lack of internal strategy" tops the list at 45% of responses. Organizations know AI is valuable but struggle with where to start, how to prioritize, and what success looks like. This is primarily a leadership and planning challenge, not a technology one.

  • No AI roadmap or vision - ad hoc tool adoption without coherent plan
  • Unclear prioritization - which use cases deliver most value?
  • Missing success metrics - how to measure ROI and progress?
  • Lack of executive alignment - different departments going different directions
  • No dedicated ownership - IT, Operations, Innovation all pointing at each other
  • Paralysis by analysis - waiting for "perfect" strategy before starting
  • This barrier is solvable with external help - consulting, frameworks, peer learning

#2 Barrier: Value Clarity Problem (36%)

36% cite "Not clear how to get value" as a barrier. This connects to the strategy gap - without clear use cases and success metrics, organizations can't confidently invest. Many are stuck in perpetual "exploration" mode.

  • ROI uncertainty - hard to quantify time savings and efficiency gains
  • Lack of industry benchmarks - "what should we expect?" question unanswered
  • Pilot project results unclear - testing without proper measurement
  • Difficulty attributing improvements - was it AI or other factors?
  • Long payback periods - implementation costs front-loaded, benefits delayed
  • Need for use case libraries - concrete examples from peer organizations
  • Organizations want to see proof before commitment

#3 & #4: People and Skills Challenges (32% & 28%)

32% cite "Employee resistance/training needs" and 28% cite "Shortage of skilled staff." The human factors matter deeply: change management, upskilling, and having champions who understand both AI and construction are critical success factors.

  • Fear of job displacement - "will AI replace me?" anxiety
  • Learning curve resistance - busy people don't want to learn new tools
  • Generational divide - younger staff embrace, older staff skeptical
  • Lack of internal expertise - no one owns AI knowledge and best practices
  • Training program gaps - no systematic upskilling approach
  • Need for AI champions - internal evangelists showing what's possible
  • Hiring challenges - can't find "construction + AI" talent combo
  • One user noted: "Older generation needs more education on AI. A basic training course would be helpful"

#5: Privacy/Security Concerns (30%)

30% cite "Privacy/security concerns" as a major barrier. Construction firms handle sensitive client data, competitive bids, and proprietary methods. Fear of data leakage to public AI models creates adoption friction, especially for larger firms.

  • Confidential client information - project details, financials, sensitive designs
  • Competitive bid data - pricing strategies that can't be leaked
  • Proprietary methods - trade secrets and internal processes
  • Regulatory compliance - GDPR, CCPA, industry-specific data rules
  • Public AI model fears - "Is my data training OpenAI's next model?"
  • Lack of governance frameworks - no clear policies on what's safe to share
  • Need for private AI deployments - on-premise or tenant-isolated solutions
  • One user: "We have an in-house ChatGPT for security concerns but its capacity is reduced"

Other Significant Barriers

  • Integration with BIM, Procore, Revit (22%): Generic AI tools don't connect to construction-specific software, limiting utility for core technical work
  • Cost of implementation (18%): Licensing, training, integration, and change management costs add up quickly
  • Regulatory/contractual uncertainty (11%): Who owns AI-generated work? Liability questions? Contract language unclear

Project Lifecycle Stages Most Benefiting from AI

Use Case Insights

Preconstruction & Estimating Lead AI Adoption (48%)

48% identify Preconstruction/Estimating as a top opportunity. This makes strategic sense - these activities are document-heavy, require analyzing RFPs and specs, involve repetitive calculations, and happen early when AI can influence bids and planning.

  • RFP analysis: Extracting requirements from 200+ page bid documents
  • Quantity takeoff acceleration: Automating measurements and counts
  • Cost estimation support: Historical data analysis and pricing validation
  • Scope of work generation: Converting specs to detailed SOWs
  • Risk identification: Flagging unusual requirements or red flags
  • Proposal assembly: Drafting technical responses and narratives
  • High ROI potential: Winning more bids or winning same bids faster
  • Early lifecycle stage means AI improvements compound through entire project

Project Management is Ripe for AI (47%)

47% see Project Management as a key AI opportunity. PMs handle massive documentation, coordination across stakeholders, meeting summaries, RFIs, change orders - all communication-heavy tasks where AI excels at drafting, summarizing, and organizing.

  • Meeting minutes and action items: Automatically generated from recordings
  • RFI responses: Drafting responses to information requests
  • Change order documentation: Describing scope changes and impacts
  • Daily/weekly reports: Summarizing progress and issues
  • Email management: Drafting, summarizing, prioritizing communications
  • Schedule analysis: Identifying critical path risks and delays
  • Document search: Finding relevant info in project archives
  • Coordination across trades: Tracking dependencies and conflicts
  • PMs spend 60%+ time on communication - AI can reclaim hours daily

Design and Safety as Emerging Areas

Design (24%) and Safety & Compliance (23%) are seen as high-potential areas, though adoption lags preconstruction and PM. Design could leverage AI for optimization and iteration; Safety for analyzing incident reports, generating toolbox talks, and ensuring compliance documentation.

  • Design optimization: Generative design, structural analysis, energy modeling
  • Code compliance checking: Automated review against building codes
  • Safety plan generation: Site-specific hazard analysis and mitigation
  • Toolbox talk creation: Daily safety briefings for crews
  • Incident report analysis: Pattern recognition in near-misses and accidents
  • Regulatory documentation: Compliance paperwork and audits
  • Both areas require specialized AI tools beyond general chatbots

Current AI Applications - What's Actually Being Done

What organizations are doing with AI today:

  • Content Creation: Proposals, presentations, marketing materials, contracts, SOWs
  • Meeting Support: Minutes, summaries, follow-up actions, transcript analysis
  • Communication: Email drafting, RFI responses, coordination correspondence
  • Analysis: Spreadsheet work, cost estimating support, building code research
  • Safety: Toolbox talk generation, safety briefings, incident documentation
  • Limited Advanced Use: Very few mention workflow automation, BIM integration, predictive capabilities, or custom AI models

The gap: Current use focuses on individual productivity (emails, documents). Future value lies in process automation (estimating systems, project controls, quality assurance).

AI is primarily being used for communication and documentation tasks today. The real transformation will come when it's integrated into technical workflows, estimating systems, and core business processes - that's where the industry is heading next.

Future Direction Insights

Strong Momentum for Increased Investment

48% rate their likelihood to increase AI investment at 7 or higher in the next 12 months. Only 16% show low interest (0-2 rating). This signals significant market opportunity for AI solutions, training, and consulting services in construction.

  • Market is ready: Nearly half of organizations planning significant investment
  • Low resistance: Only 16% skeptical or uninterested
  • Neutral middle (18%): Persuadable with right proof points and support
  • Investment correlates with maturity: Higher maturity higher investment intent
  • Window of opportunity: Organizations that invest now gain 12-24 month lead
  • Budget allocation increasing: AI moving from "innovation fund" to operating budget

Where Transformation Will Happen First

Departments/areas most likely for AI transformation:

  • Project Management (18%): Most frequently cited - high documentation burden
  • Estimating/Preconstruction (15%): High-value, document-intensive, competitive advantage
  • Business Development/Marketing (12%): Content creation and proposal automation
  • Administration/Back Office (10%): Repetitive documentation, data entry tasks
  • Proposals Team (8%): RFP responses and technical writing specialists
  • Operations (7%): Process optimization and workflow improvement
  • Virtual Construction/BIM (6%): For more advanced/larger organizations
  • Human Resources (5%): Recruitment and onboarding automation
  • Accounting/Finance (4%): Invoice processing, AR/AP automation

#1 Support Need: Finding the Right Tools (63%)

63% need help finding the best AI tools for their needs. The market is overwhelming - thousands of AI tools, unclear differentiation, difficulty assessing fit. Organizations want guidance on construction-specific solutions that integrate with their existing systems.

  • Tool proliferation overwhelms: Too many options, unclear differences
  • Generic vs. specialized: Should we use ChatGPT or construction-specific AI?
  • Integration requirements: Tools must work with Procore, Revit, BIM 360, etc.
  • Vendor evaluation difficulty: How to assess capabilities and reliability?
  • Total cost confusion: Licensing + implementation + training costs unclear
  • Need for curated marketplace: Pre-vetted, construction-focused AI tools
  • Opportunity for partnerships: Tool providers + implementation consultants

#2: Industry Insights & Best Practices (50%)

50% want to know what peers are doing with AI. Strong demand for case studies, benchmarks, and learning from others' successes and failures. "What are other general contractors achieving?" "What's working in engineering firms?"

  • Peer learning preferred: Trust experiences from similar organizations
  • ROI proof points needed: Concrete examples of time/cost savings
  • Implementation lessons: What worked? What didn't? What would you do differently?
  • Benchmarking desired: How do we compare to industry standards?
  • Use case library value: "If they can do it, we can too"
  • Community building opportunity: AI user groups, roundtables, forums
  • Opportunity for industry reports and research (like this survey!)

#3: Training Programs (46%)

46% need training programs to upskill teams on effective AI use. Gap between tool availability and user capability. People have access but don't know prompt engineering, workflow integration, or best practices.

  • Prompt engineering skills: How to get better outputs from AI
  • Workflow integration: When and how to use AI in daily work
  • Role-specific training: AI for estimators AI for project managers
  • Hands-on workshops: Practice with real scenarios, not just theory
  • Ongoing education: AI evolves fast, need continuous learning
  • Certification programs: Validate AI skills for career advancement
  • Train-the-trainer models: Build internal AI champions

#4: Strategy Support (25%)

25% need strategy support to get started and find highest-impact projects. Help with AI roadmap development, use case prioritization, success metrics definition, and change management planning.

  • AI roadmap development: Where to start? What's the sequence?
  • Use case prioritization: Which projects deliver most value fastest?
  • Success metrics definition: How to measure ROI and progress?
  • Change management planning: Getting organization bought in
  • Governance frameworks: Policies, procedures, risk management
  • Budget planning: How much to invest? Where to allocate?
  • Opportunity for AI strategy consulting specifically for construction

#5: Policy & Governance (25%)

25% need help with policy and governance work, particularly around privacy-centric AI use. How to enable AI adoption while protecting sensitive data and maintaining compliance?

  • Data classification frameworks: What data is safe for AI? What isn't?
  • Privacy policies: GDPR, CCPA, industry-specific requirements
  • Usage guidelines: When is AI appropriate? When should humans lead?
  • Security protocols: How to deploy AI safely in enterprise?
  • Contractual language: How to address AI in client contracts?
  • Liability considerations: Who's responsible for AI errors?
  • Ethical guidelines: Responsible AI use in construction
The industry is ready to move from experimentation to implementation. Success will depend on strategic guidance, practical training, and construction-specific tools that integrate with existing workflows rather than replacing them.
